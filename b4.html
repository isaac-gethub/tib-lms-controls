Section 1 · The Cost of Getting Controls Wrong

Imagine a client who has invested between six months and three years in an SAP S/4HANA transformation. At go-live, they expect the system to work and they expect to run their business with confidence the very next morning. Now consider what happens if controls are not embedded before go-live. A controls failure discovered post-live is not an abstract risk — it is rework, cost, and potential regulatory exposure at exactly the moment when an organisation is most vulnerable.

⚠️
What the Source Session Says
The transcript is direct: "if they don't have their risks and controls right, there may be consequences — financial misstatements or loss, or they may have to end up redesigning their processes after go-live." Three concrete consequences: misstatements, post-live rework, and the inability to unlock the new value that S/4HANA was intended to deliver.

Compliant on Day One

The source session identifies the primary objective precisely: clients should be compliant on day one of go-live. That phrase captures the essential value proposition. When you embed controls during implementation, you are not creating compliance overhead — you are eliminating a far more expensive problem: discovering control gaps after the system is live, after real transactions have been processed, after external auditors have begun their review.

Section 2 · Four Value Drivers

The source session identifies four value drivers that justify a controls workstream in an S/4HANA transformation programme. These are the arguments that land with a programme sponsor, a CFO, or an audit committee — because they are framed in business outcomes, not in controls taxonomy.

Value Driver 01
Issues Prevented from Go-Live
The preventive measure — every financial, operational, and regulatory risk identified before go-live is a risk that cannot manifest as a live-system failure. Every SoD conflict resolved during design is a conflict that cannot generate a payment fraud or a material weakness finding in the live system.
Outcome: Fewer post-live findings
Value Driver 02
Designing the Process Right the First Time
Counter the lift-and-shift instinct. The source session is direct: "that is not the mindset we should be going with." The moment of transformation is the cheapest possible time to redesign a process — far cheaper than redesigning it post-live. Build compliance in, not on.
Outcome: No re-engineering post-live
Value Driver 03
Reduced Cost of Compliance
Controls designed, documented, and tested from day one require less auditor testing time than controls hastily assembled to address findings. Higher audit reliance means smaller sample sizes, lower external audit fees, and fewer audit hours consumed each year over the life of the programme.
Outcome: Lower annual audit cost
Value Driver 04
Integration and Efficiency
Controls fully embedded and operationalised in the client's environment — not delivered as a document set and left. The goal is controls the business owns, automates where possible, and can continuously improve over time. A controls workstream that leaves behind sustainable processes delivers value long after the engagement closes.
Outcome: Sustainable controls maturity

Section 3 · The External Audit Connection

The source session makes the external audit connection explicit: controls are the inputs for external auditors to conduct their annual audit. When control gaps surface during pre-go-live, the programme team can detect them, fix them, and address them so that once the actual audit starts, the client faces less cost and governs their controls framework more effectively.

🔍
The Direct Line to Audit Cost
A control that is designed, documented, tested, and operating from go-live requires far less auditor testing than a control assembled post-finding. The difference is not marginal — it compounds over multiple audit cycles. The controls workstream is, in effect, reducing the client's external audit cost every year for the life of the S/4HANA system.
🎯 The Practitioner's Frame
"Value Protection, Not Compliance Overhead"
When you enter a client engagement, you are not there to build documents and run reports. You are there to protect the client's investment in their transformation. Every risk you identify, every control you design, every test you execute contributes to the four value drivers. That framing — value protection, not compliance overhead — is how you speak to senior stakeholders, and it is the language that unlocks the access and the buy-in you need to do the work effectively.

Key Definitions
TermCompliant on Day One
The primary value objective of a controls workstream in an S/4HANA transformation. At the point of go-live, the client's risk and controls framework is fully designed, validated, and tested — so the organisation can begin operations in a compliant state without a post-live remediation sprint. Prevention during implementation costs a fraction of remediation after go-live.
TermLift-and-Shift
The tendency to carry the old process directly into the new system without redesign. The source session identifies this as "not the mindset we should be going with." S/4HANA transformation is the optimal moment to redesign processes for compliance, automation, and efficiency — that opportunity closes at go-live. A controls workstream explicitly challenges lift-and-shift thinking.
TermValue Protection
The practitioner's framing for the controls workstream: not compliance overhead, but protection of the client's investment in their transformation. Every control designed and tested before go-live protects the ROI of the S/4HANA investment by reducing post-live rework, lowering audit cost, and enabling sustainable operations from day one.
ConsequenceFinancial Misstatements or Loss
When control gaps are not identified pre-live, errors and fraud can occur in the production system before they are detected. This can result in financial statements that do not accurately reflect the organisation's position — a SOX material weakness, an external audit finding, and potentially a restatement.
ConsequencePost-Live Process Redesign
The most expensive consequence of missing controls at go-live. Redesigning a process after the system is live requires system changes, re-testing, business disruption, and often significant consulting expenditure — far exceeding the cost of getting the design right during implementation. The controls workstream prevents this.
