The S/4HANA Transformation Lifecycle

The S/4HANA transformation engagement is the most complex context for a controls practitioner — and the most important. The controls workstream runs across four distinct phases, each with different activities, different deliverables, and different primary coordination points. 🧭
Establish the Risk Universe
Review programme scope and business processes. Produce a preliminary risk assessment across financial, operational, and compliance risk categories. This is the foundation — without it, the RACM has no anchor.
📋
Draft the Preliminary RACM
Not a finished document — a starting point. The source session: "it will not be perfect, but it sets the ground and foundation." Every subsequent phase refines it.
🤝
Make Friends
Build relationships with SI functional leads, testing team, security team, data team, cybersecurity team. These relationships will be called upon in every subsequent phase. Invest in them now.
🎯
Influence the Future State Design
Peak influence window. Identify processes that are currently manual but could be automated in S/4HANA. Raise them now — once Key Design Decisions are finalised, influencing the design becomes progressively harder and more expensive.
🤖
AI Agent Opportunity
AI agents can perform current-state to future-state gap analysis and propose preliminary controls from programme artefacts. The practitioner's role shifts from information processing to judgement — validating, challenging, contextualising.
Phase 2 Design and Build RACM: Refined & Validated
📄
Read Functional Design Documents — Risk First
Three-step approach: (1) What risk am I concerned about? (2) Does this FD affect that risk? (3) If yes — what are the data frequency, direction, and error handling details? The error handling mechanism is typically the control.
🏗
Design Controls, Validate with Business
Translate FD analysis into specific controls. Hold working sessions with business process owners to validate — the business must agree to perform these controls on go-live. Update the RACM with specific, validated control descriptions.
⚖️
Maintain Scope Boundary
The controls team consumes programme artefacts — we do not configure anything in SAP and do not perform design work. That is the SI's role. Our deliverable is the RACM. Keeping this scope boundary clear protects independence and audit credibility.
🤖
AI Agent Opportunity
AI agents can accelerate FD analysis across hundreds of documents — extracting risk-relevant sections and proposing draft controls. The practitioner validates and refines. Volume challenge in Design and Build is the clearest ROI case for AI assistance.
Phase 3 Testing RACM: Test Evidence Captured
⚙️
Configuration Testing
Validate system settings that implement a control — OMR6 tolerance keys, workflow thresholds, approval routing. Confirm parameters are set correctly, not just that the control exists.
💳
Transactional Testing
Execute real or simulated transactions to confirm the control responds as designed — run a payment to a fictitious vendor to confirm the SoD block fires, post an invoice exceeding tolerance to confirm the system blocks it.
📊
Report Testing
Confirm exception reports are complete, accurate, and cover the right population. Run the UAR report and verify it captures all relevant user-role combinations. Agree to the underlying system data.
🎯
Testing Captain Strategy
From a major energy company engagement: integrate controls requirements into programme test cases before testing begins. Find the testing captain — the person coordinating a group of testers — and make them your key contact. Route all evidence requests and rework through one coordinator.
🔀
SIT vs UAT Scope Decision
Configuration controls (automated, no human intervention) → System Integration Testing. Workflow-driven and report-driven controls (require human review) → User Acceptance Testing. This distributes effort and aligns each control type with the most appropriate environment.
Phase 4 Cutover and Deploy RACM: Final — Go-Live Ready
✅
Finalise the RACM
Every control has an assigned owner. Descriptions reflect the tested final state. RACM is formally approved and distributed to all post-go-live stakeholders. This is the finished version of a document that began as a preliminary draft in SolCon.
🎓
Train the Control Owners
Every person responsible for a control must understand: what to do, how often, what evidence to produce, and how to escalate exceptions — especially for ITDM and manual controls, where the human element is the control.
🔍
External Auditor Confirmation Gate
The auditor must confirm the controls designed and tested are the controls they will rely on in the annual audit. This confirmation is the final gate before go-live. Touchpoint frequency increases significantly in this phase.
🗂
Maintain RACM Traceability
The RACM is not a point-in-time document — it is an iterated artefact that has been through multiple versions since SolCon. The ability to trace from the final go-live control back to the original risk assessment is important for audit credibility and future engagements.

Phase-by-Phase Detail

Expand each phase for the full controls workstream narrative.

Solution Confirmation is the first phase of the S/4HANA transformation. At this stage the client and their system implementer are defining the future state — what the new system will look like, what processes will change, and what key design decisions will be made. For the controls team, this is the most important phase of the entire engagement — not because the heaviest work happens here, but because the foundation you lay in Solution Confirmation determines everything that follows.

Three Core Activities

1. Establish the Risk Universe. Before a single control is designed, you need to understand what could go wrong in the future state. Review the programme scope, understand the business processes being transformed, and produce a preliminary risk assessment.

2. Draft the Preliminary RACM. Not a finished document — a starting point. The source session is clear: it will not be perfect, but it sets the ground and foundation for what the client's risk and controls landscape will look like. Every subsequent phase refines it.

3. Make friends. You need relationships with the system implementer's functional leads, the testing team, the security team, the data team, and the cybersecurity team. These relationships will be called upon in every subsequent phase. Invest in them now, when the programme is still in planning mode.

🎯
Peak Influence Window
Solution Confirmation is your best opportunity to influence the future state design. If you identify a process that is currently manual but could be automated in S/4HANA, raise it now. Once the Key Design Decisions are finalised and the system is being configured, influencing the design becomes progressively harder and more expensive. The source session notes: it is better to have a workflow designed and built now than to try to get it built later in the programme.
🤖 AI Agent Callout — Solution Confirmation
AI agents are beginning to add value in Solution Confirmation — performing current-state to future-state gap analysis, processing programme artefacts, and proposing preliminary controls. In a phase where the team is consuming large volumes of process documentation with limited bandwidth, the efficiency gain from AI-assisted analysis can be significant. The practitioner's role shifts from raw information processing to judgement: validating, challenging, and contextualising what the agent proposes.

Design and Build is the phase of heavy lifting. The client's system implementer is now configuring the system, building workflows, and producing the functional design documents and configuration rationale that define exactly how the future state will work. The controls team's role is to consume those artefacts and translate them into controls.

Reading Functional Design Documents — Risk First

When you receive a functional design document, your job is not to read it word for word. Your job is to identify the risk relevant to your RACM. The three-step approach:

Step 1 — What risk am I concerned about? Financial, operational, or regulatory. Define this before opening the document — it is your filter.

Step 2 — Does this FD affect that risk? If a failed interface could cause a financial misstatement or a compliance exposure, the FD is relevant. If not, move on.

Step 3 — If relevant: extract the key parameters. For interface controls: data direction and frequency, source and target systems, and critically — the error handling mechanism. Does the system detect interface errors, alert someone, and trigger resolution? That error handling mechanism is the control.

⚠️
Scope Boundary — Critical
The controls team does not configure anything in SAP and does not perform design work. That is the system implementer's role. The controls team consumes the programme's design artefacts and produces the RACM. Keeping these roles distinct protects independence and audit credibility — the controls team reviewing the SI's design work is only meaningful if they are genuinely independent of that design.
🤖 AI Agent Callout — Design and Build
Design and Build presents the clearest ROI case for AI assistance in the controls workstream. The team may be working through hundreds of functional design documents across multiple business processes, multiple team members, and a compressed timeline. AI agents can accelerate initial FD analysis — identifying risk-relevant sections, extracting error handling language, and proposing draft control descriptions. The practitioner validates and refines. The work shifts from document triage to control judgement.

Testing is the phase where you find out whether the controls you designed will actually work. The source session describes this as the best and last chance to see if the controls will operate as expected. Three test categories define the controls testing approach.

Configuration Testing
What it tests
System settings that implement a control — confirms parameters are set correctly, not just that the control exists.
🔍 Example: Inspect OMR6 tolerance keys; confirm threshold ≤3%; verify no vendor-specific overrides.
Transactional Testing
What it tests
Control responds as designed when a transaction is executed — positive and negative scenarios.
🔍 Example: Run payment to fictitious vendor — confirm SoD block fires. Post invoice exceeding tolerance — confirm system blocks.
Report Testing
What it tests
Exception reports are complete, accurate, and include the right data population.
🔍 Example: Run UAR report — verify all relevant user-role combinations are captured; agree to underlying system data.
🏭 Field Example — Major Energy Company Engagement
The strategy that worked on a major energy company engagement: map controls to the programme's existing test cases before testing begins. Do not wait until the testing team has written their scripts — review the test case steps and add controls testing requirements directly. When a tester opens the test case, they see the programme requirement and the controls requirement in the same document. Also: find the testing captain — the person coordinating a group of testers — and make them your key contact. Routing evidence requests and rework through one coordinator is far more efficient than chasing individual testers across thousands of emails.

SIT vs UAT Scope Decision

System Integration Testing: Configuration controls — those that operate without human intervention — belong here, where the system itself is being validated.

User Acceptance Testing: Workflow-driven and report-driven controls — those requiring human review and action — belong here, where business users are in the system. This distributes the testing workload across cycles and aligns each control type with the most appropriate testing environment.

Cutover and Deploy is the final phase before go-live. The system has been configured, controls have been designed, and testing has confirmed they work. Now the question is: is the organisation ready to operate those controls from day one?

Three Go-Live Readiness Activities

1. Finalise the RACM. Confirm every control has an assigned owner. Descriptions reflect the tested final state. The RACM is formally approved and distributed to everyone who will need it post-go-live. This is the completed version of a document that began as a preliminary draft in Solution Confirmation.

2. Train the control owners. Every person responsible for a control — particularly ITDM and manual controls — must understand what they need to do, how often, what evidence to produce, and how to escalate exceptions. The source session is explicit: people must know how to execute the controls before the system goes live.

3. External auditor confirmation gate. Across the transformation, the external auditor has been a periodic touchpoint. At Cutover and Deploy, the frequency of those touchpoints increases significantly. The auditor must confirm that the controls the programme designed and tested are the controls they will rely on in their annual audit. That confirmation is the final gate before go-live.

🗂
RACM Traceability — The Final Principle
The RACM is not a point-in-time document — it is an iterated artefact that has been through multiple versions since the preliminary draft in Solution Confirmation. The ability to trace from the final tested control back to the original risk assessment that motivated it is important for audit credibility, for programme retrospectives, and for future engagements with the same client. Maintain version control on the RACM from day one.

Key Definitions
TermRACM (Risk and Controls Matrix)
The primary deliverable of the controls workstream in a transformation engagement. Progressively refined across all four phases: Preliminary Draft (SolCon) → Refined and Validated (Design and Build) → Test Evidence Captured (Testing) → Final Go-Live Ready (Cutover and Deploy). Each row maps a risk to its control, owner, test procedure, and evidence status.
TermFunctional Design Document (FD)
A programme artefact produced by the system implementer that describes how a specific function or process will work in the future state system. Controls teams read FDs to identify risk-relevant design decisions and extract control parameters — particularly error handling mechanisms for interface controls, and workflow thresholds for approval controls.
TermTesting Captain
A coordinator within the testing programme who manages a group of testers. In the controls testing context, the testing captain is the practitioner's primary contact for evidence requests, rework, and test case integration. Routing communications through one coordinator rather than individual testers is significantly more efficient on large programmes.
TermSIT vs UAT Scope Decision
The decision about which controls to test in System Integration Testing (SIT) versus User Acceptance Testing (UAT). Configuration controls — automated, no human intervention — are scoped for SIT where the system is being validated. Workflow-driven and report-driven controls requiring human action are scoped for UAT where business users are in the system.
TermExternal Auditor Confirmation Gate
At Cutover and Deploy, the external auditor must confirm that the controls the programme designed and tested are the controls they will rely on in the annual audit. This is a formal prerequisite for go-live — not an optional step. Touchpoints with the external auditor increase in frequency as go-live approaches. Audit reliance on day one depends on this confirmation.
TermScope Boundary (Controls vs SI)
The controls team consumes programme artefacts and produces the RACM — they do not configure SAP and do not perform design work. That is the system implementer's role. This scope boundary protects the independence of the controls review and maintains the audit credibility of the RACM as an independent assessment of the future-state design.
