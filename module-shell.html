Section 1 · Preventive Controls

A preventive control is one that stops an undesirable event from occurring in the first place. It operates upstream — before the error, before the fraud, before the non-compliant transaction. In SAP environments, preventive controls are your first and most powerful line of defence.

🛡
The Practitioner's Definition
A preventive control is stopping the activity from even happening. This is the gold standard in control design — you are not waiting for something to go wrong and then reacting. You are making it structurally impossible for the adverse event to occur in the first place.

1.1 · Access Restriction

01
Access Restriction via Role Design
Preventive

By configuring roles and authorisations so that users can only perform the transactions required for their job function, you prevent Segregation of Duties conflicts from materialising. A user who cannot access XK01 (Vendor Create) and F110 (Payment Run) in the same role simply cannot commit vendor fraud — the system will not permit it.

SAP Mechanism: Authorisation object-level role design
Detection tool: SAP GRC Access Control Ruleset
⚠️
GRC Detects, Role Design Prevents
The SAP GRC Ruleset is an analytical engine — it tells you whether access restrictions are working. But it is the role design itself, enforced at the authorisation object level, that is the preventive control. In an RCM, the control is "Vendor Create and Payment Run access segregated in role design." GRC Access Control is the test procedure.

1.2 · Workflow Enforcement

02
Workflow Enforcement — MSMP Approval
Preventive

SAP's workflow engine can be configured to require sequential approvals before a transaction completes. In Procure-to-Pay, a purchase order above a defined threshold cannot be released unless an authorised approver has electronically confirmed it. The process literally cannot advance without the approval — the system enforces the control, not a person's memory or goodwill.

1.3 · System Configuration Validation

03
Three-Way Match — OMR6 Tolerance Configuration
Preventive

The three-way match in Materials Management is a configured preventive control. The system automatically compares the purchase order, goods receipt, and vendor invoice amounts. If the discrepancy exceeds the configured tolerance in OMR6, the system blocks the invoice from posting. No human intervention is required — it operates consistently on every single transaction, every time.

Configuration: OMR6 tolerance key settings
Process: ME21N → MIGO → MIRO
💡
Audit Testing Implication
When testing a configured preventive control like the three-way match, you must inspect the OMR6 settings and verify the tolerance parameters are set appropriately — not just confirm the control "exists." A tolerance of 99% means the control provides almost no protection. This is the audit implication of Ronnie's distinction, covered in Section 3.
Section 2

Section 2 · Detective Controls

Preventive controls are the ideal. But we cannot prevent everything. Some risks are too complex to fully prevent through system configuration alone, some involve human judgement that cannot be automated, and some control gaps are only discovered after the fact. This is where detective controls come in.

A detective control does not stop an event from occurring. Instead, it identifies that something has gone wrong after the fact, enabling corrective action to be taken. Think of detective controls as your second line of defence — a fail-safe for situations where prevention was not possible or did not hold.

2.1 · Management Review of Exception Reports

01
Exception Report Review
Detective

At the end of a defined period, a responsible manager reviews a system-generated report of unusual or potentially erroneous transactions. The event has already occurred — the control's purpose is to identify it and trigger a corrective response. In SAP, this typically covers journal entries posted after period-end cutoff, vendor master changes in the prior month, or payment runs that bypassed standard approval workflows.

SAP Reports: FB03 · FK04 · F110 log
Frequency: Weekly / Monthly / Quarterly

2.2 · User Access Review

02
User Access Review (UAR)
Detective

On a quarterly or annual cycle, business process owners review active users and their assigned roles. Inappropriate access — due to role change, leavers, or SoD policy violations — is flagged for removal. The UAR does not prevent the access from existing during the period under review. But it is a critical second fail-safe: if access provisioning controls failed, the UAR will surface the problem before it becomes a sustained exposure or an audit finding.

Tool: SAP GRC Access Control — user access certification
Frequency: Quarterly or Annual
One of the most commonly tested controls in a SOX 404 engagement

2.3 · Reconciliation

03
Reconciliation Controls
Detective

Reconciliation compares two independent data sets and identifies discrepancies. A discrepancy signals that something happened upstream — a posting error, an unauthorised journal entry, or a process breakdown — that must be investigated and corrected. In SAP, reconciliation controls frequently run through the Financial Closing Cockpit and are supported by automated variance reports, making most of them ITDM controls (see Section 3).

Types: Bank recon · Subledger-to-GL · Intercompany
SAP: FF67 · FBL3N · FBU3

🎯
Design Rule
Design preventive first. Detective is your second line of defence — not a substitute. On every engagement, push clients toward preventive controls wherever the process allows. When prevention is not feasible, implement detective controls with defined frequency, clear ownership, and documented evidence of review.
Section 3

Section 3 · Ronnie's Distinction — Automated vs. Configured

Within the broader category of automated or configured controls, there is a meaningful difference between two sub-types that practitioners frequently conflate. A colleague, Ronnie, flagged this distinction in a field session — and it is one of those insights that separates experienced practitioners from entry-level candidates.

🔑 Ronnie's Field Insight
"An automated control could be based on system configuration, or that control could be inherent to the system — an out-of-the-box standard control. There is a distinction."
— Ronnie, SAP GRC Practitioner · Source Session AUD-20260602-WA0018

A configured control is enabled by deliberate system configuration — someone has set it up. It only works if the parameters are set correctly. The three-way match tolerance in OMR6 is the canonical example: powerful, but only if the tolerance threshold is set appropriately. A tolerance of 99% technically means the control is configured, but operationally it provides almost no protection.

⚠️
Audit Testing Implication
Configured controls require a configuration review. You must inspect the OMR6 settings and verify the tolerance parameters are appropriate. Simply confirming "three-way match is turned on" is insufficient evidence.

An inherent automated control is active by default — it operates regardless of whether the client has done anything special. SAP's built-in duplicate invoice check in MIRO posting is a clear example: the system automatically checks for duplicate document numbers and vendor-invoice combinations on every posting, out-of-the-box.

💡
Audit Testing Implication
Inherent controls do not require configuration verification. But you must confirm the standard functionality has not been switched off — check the relevant system switches. Some clients disable standard checks to accommodate a workaround; that disablement is a control gap requiring a compensating control.

3.1 · The ITDM Control — IT-Dependent Manual

Between fully automated controls and fully manual controls sits the IT-Dependent Manual (ITDM) control. An ITDM leverages the system to generate data — a report, a log, an exception list — but requires a human to review that output and take action.

💻
System Component
IT-Generated Data
The system generates the report, log, or exception list. Examples: SAP exception report, GRC SoD conflict list, Financial Closing Cockpit variance output.
+
👤
Human Component
Manual Review & Action
A competent person reviews the output, investigates exceptions, and takes documented corrective action. Both steps must be evidenced for the control to be considered effective.
⚠️
ITDM Testing Requirement — Both Elements
When testing an ITDM, you must test both elements: (1) verify the report is complete and accurate, and (2) verify that a competent person reviewed it and took documented action on exceptions. Missing either element renders the ITDM control ineffective.

3.2 · The Control Nature Hierarchy

① Highest Reliability
Configured / Automated
System enforces on every transaction automatically. No human action required in normal operation.
Examples: OMR6 tolerance · MSMP workflow · Role design · Duplicate invoice check
First Design Choice
② Medium Reliability
IT-Dependent Manual (ITDM)
System generates data; human must review and act. Reliability depends on review quality and frequency.
Examples: Exception report review · UAR · Reconciliation
Second Choice
③ Lowest Reliability
Manual Control
Fully human — no system component. Most costly to test, maintain, and rely upon. Subject to human error.
Examples: Paper invoice review · Physical inventory count · Verbal approval
Last Resort
✅
The Practitioner's Value-Add
Wherever possible, encourage clients to move up the hierarchy during system implementation. A manual control converted to a configured automated control is not just a compliance improvement — it is an operational efficiency gain and a long-term reduction in audit cost. That is a direct contribution to their controls maturity.

Key Definitions
TermPreventive Control
A control that stops an undesirable event from occurring before it happens. Operates upstream of the risk event. Highest reliance in an audit context because it blocks every in-scope transaction. In SAP: role design, workflow enforcement, and system configuration validation are the three primary types.
TermDetective Control
A control that identifies an undesirable event after it has occurred, enabling corrective action. Effectiveness depends on frequency, quality of review, and completeness of system output. Never a substitute for preventive controls — always a second line of defence.
TermConfigured vs. Inherent Automated Control
Configured: enabled by deliberate system setup (OMR6 three-way match). Requires configuration review — auditor must verify parameters are appropriate.

Inherent: active by default as standard SAP functionality (MIRO duplicate invoice check). Does not require configuration verification, but auditor must confirm it has not been switched off.
TermIT-Dependent Manual (ITDM)
A control that leverages IT to generate data but requires a human to review and act. Testing requires evidence of both: (1) the system output is complete and accurate, and (2) a competent person reviewed it and acted on exceptions. Most exception report reviews, UARs, and reconciliation controls are ITDM in practice.
TermMSMP Workflow
Multi-Step Multi-Process workflow in SAP GRC. The standard implementation vehicle for sequential approval workflows in S/4HANA. Used for access request approvals, PO approvals, and other process-level approval chains. A preventive control — the process cannot advance without the approval being electronically recorded.
