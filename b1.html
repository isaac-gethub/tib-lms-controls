Section 1 · The Risk Universe

When you arrive on a new SAP engagement — whether it is a greenfield S/4HANA implementation, an operating effectiveness review, or a remediation programme — the first task is establishing the Risk Universe. What are all the things that could go wrong in this environment? Until you have that picture, you cannot design controls, scope tests, or give your client a credible view of their exposure. Risk classification is how we build that picture systematically.

🧭
The Practitioner's Starting Question
Every controls engagement — regardless of industry, SAP version, or client size — begins with one question: what could go wrong? Your answer to that question is your Risk Universe. This module gives you the framework to answer it precisely and consistently.

1.1 · Financial Risk

Financial risk arises when an error, omission, or deliberate manipulation results in a financial statement that does not accurately reflect the organisation's true position. This is the category most directly linked to fraud, misstatement, and external audit findings — and it is the primary target of SAP GRC Access Control deployments.

Consider a concrete SAP example. A user creates a fictitious vendor using transaction XK01 — Vendor Master Create — and then processes a payment run to that vendor via F110 — Automatic Payment Programme. Because the same person can both create the payee and authorise the payment, no independent check exists. Cash leaves the organisation, accounts payable is misstated, and the external auditor will raise a material weakness finding under SOX Section 404.

HIGH SoD Conflict — Vendor Creation + Payment Run
Function A XK01  Create / Maintain Vendor Master
Function B F110  Automatic Payment Run
Risk One user can create a fictitious vendor and pay it — no independent authorisation required
Control SoD Ruleset in SAP GRC Access Control · MSMP workflow approval for exceptions
COSO Obj. Operations · Financial Reporting
💡
GRC Connection
Financial risk is the primary driver for deploying SAP GRC Access Control. The GRC Ruleset is essentially a codified library of financial risks expressed as SoD conflict rules. When GRC surfaces a conflict, it is surfacing an elevated inherent risk awaiting a control response.

1.2 · Compliance Risk

Compliance risk is the risk that the organisation fails to meet an external regulatory obligation. In SAP environments, this covers a wide spectrum of requirements depending on the organisation's sector and geography:

US Listed Companies
SOX Section 404
Management must assess and external auditors must attest to the effectiveness of internal controls over financial reporting. SAP is the system of record — control gaps in SAP become SOX material weaknesses.
EU / Global
GDPR
Personal data stored and processed in SAP — HR records, customer data, supplier contacts — must be handled per GDPR Article requirements. Inadequate access controls or audit logging creates compliance exposure.
Life Sciences
FDA 21 CFR Part 11
Electronic records and electronic signatures in SAP must meet FDA validation standards. Failure to maintain compliant audit trails is a direct regulatory violation with enforcement consequences.
All Sectors
IFRS / Local GAAP
SAP is the system of record for financial reporting. Configuration errors in chart of accounts, consolidation settings, or posting period controls can create IFRS / GAAP non-compliance.
⚠️
The System of Record Multiplier
Because SAP is the system of record, weaknesses in configuration, access controls, or change management can translate directly into regulatory non-compliance. The consequences extend beyond financial penalties to reputational damage that controls remediation cannot easily reverse.

1.3 · Operational Risk

Operational risk arises from weaknesses in day-to-day processes, system configuration, or human behaviour that impair the organisation's ability to operate accurately and efficiently. A key SAP example is the three-way match tolerance setting in Materials Management.

In a correctly configured Procure-to-Pay process, the system automatically compares three documents before posting an invoice:

ME21N
Purchase Order
Agreed price & quantity for goods or services
→
MIGO
Goods Receipt
Physical confirmation goods were received
→
MIRO
Vendor Invoice
Supplier's invoice — must match PO & GR within tolerance
⚠
FAIL
Invoice Posts
Tolerance too wide — discrepancy ignored

If the tolerance threshold is configured too loosely — or not configured at all — the system will post and pay invoices with significant discrepancies between purchase order value, goods receipt quantity, and vendor invoice amount. This is not necessarily fraud: it may simply be a configuration weakness. But it costs the organisation money and erodes the reliability of its financial data — a textbook operational risk with direct financial impact.

Operational risk also includes inadequate training (users bypassing standard process steps), unclear process ownership (no one reviews exception reports), and system availability failures. In the context of controls audit work, we focus primarily on process-level operational risks that can be mitigated by automated or manual controls.

1.4 · The Overlap — Risks Are Not Independent Silos

The three categories do not operate independently. A single control weakness can simultaneously create Financial, Compliance, and Operational risk. This overlap is one of the most important insights for practitioners — and the reason risk classification must identify the primary risk driver rather than simply listing all affected categories.

Section 2

Section 2 · From Risk to Control

Now that we have our Risk Universe mapped, the question becomes: what do we do about it? The answer is a control. A control is an activity to mitigate or manage a risk — something put in place to address the risks we have identified. That definition is simple. Making it operational requires one additional concept.

2.1 · Inherent Risk and Residual Risk

⚡
Inherent Risk
The level of risk present before any controls are applied. Your baseline when you first assess a client environment.
🛡
Controls
The gap-filler. Your primary deliverable on an engagement. Moves the organisation from inherent to residual.
✓
Residual Risk
What remains after controls are implemented and operating. Must fall within the organisation's defined risk tolerance.
Conceptual Formula Residual Risk = Inherent Risk − Control Effectiveness
This is a conceptual model, not a literal arithmetic equation. In practice, practitioners use risk ratings (High / Medium / Low) adjusted by the degree of reliance placed on each control, informed by design and operating effectiveness testing results.

Your job on an engagement is to help your client move from an elevated inherent-risk position to an acceptable residual-risk position. That gap is filled by controls. This is why risk identification and control design always move together. Risk identification without control design is just a problem list. Control design without risk identification is a set of activities with no anchor.

2.2 · The SAP GRC Ruleset — The Risk-to-Control Cycle

SAP GRC Access Control's Ruleset is the most direct example of the risk-to-control cycle in an SAP context. Each rule in the Ruleset says: if a single user can perform Function A and Function B, a Segregation of Duties conflict exists. The Ruleset is a codified Risk Universe for access-related financial risks.

01
Run SoD Analysis
The GRC Ruleset scans all user roles and identifies combinations of transactions that create SoD conflicts. Each conflict is a flagged inherent risk.
02
Classify the Risk
Each conflict is rated High, Medium, or Low based on the financial exposure of the conflicting functions. XK01 + F110 is typically High — direct payment fraud potential.
03
Design the Control Response
Options: (a) remove conflicting access from the role, (b) implement a compensating control such as an MSMP approval workflow, or (c) formally accept the risk with documented business justification and enhanced monitoring.
04
Measure Residual Risk
Re-run the SoD analysis. Remaining unmitigated conflicts represent residual risk. Test compensating controls for operating effectiveness. Document the outcome in the Risk and Controls Matrix.
Risk Function A Function B Exposure
HIGH XK01 Create Vendor F110 Payment Run Fictitious vendor fraud
HIGH FB01 Post Journal FB03 Approve Journal Unauthorised journal entry
MED ME21N Create PO MIRO Post Invoice Bypass PO approval workflow
MED PA30 Maintain HR Master PC00 Run Payroll Ghost employee / payroll fraud
MED SE16N Table Browse SM30 Table Maintain Direct table manipulation

PetroNova S.A., a mid-size hydrocarbon producer headquartered in Port Meridian, Arcadia, commissioned a 37-week SAP S/4HANA implementation with TIB Systems as the QA/QC partner. During Phase 0 — Blueprint & Governance, the controls audit team produced a Risk Universe document covering all three risk categories for each process area.

Key finding in Accounts Payable: A single user role combined vendor master maintenance (XK01, XK02) with payment run authorisation (F110). The inherent risk rating was High — direct payment fraud potential. The recommended control was a workflow-enforced dual approval in SAP, implemented in MSMP. Post-control, residual risk was reduced to Low, subject to annual operating effectiveness testing.

This single finding — surfaced in Blueprint, remediated in Design, tested in UAT — prevented a potential control failure from reaching production on Day 1 of Go-Live. That is the value proposition of a controls-first implementation approach.

Section 3 · Classification in Practice — The RCM

Before we close, one point about classification that practitioners sometimes underestimate: it drives everything that follows. The risk classification you assign in the first column of your Risk and Controls Matrix determines the control, the test procedure, and the finding.

Financial Risk
Substantive Procedures
Transaction testing, balance validation, journal entry review. Test what happened in the system — not just whether a control exists.
Compliance Risk
Regulatory Standard Test
Test against the specific regulatory requirement — SOX 404, a GDPR article, or an FDA standard. Internal policy alone is not sufficient.
Operational Risk
Process Walkthrough
Configuration review, process observation, tolerance threshold checks. Test whether the process runs as designed under real conditions.

The Practitioner's Compass
Risk classification is the practitioner's compass. It orients every subsequent decision. On every engagement — implementation QA/QC, operating effectiveness review, or remediation — the moment you correctly classify a risk, the right control, the right test, and the right finding all follow naturally. Get classification wrong and your entire effort is misdirected. This is why it is Module B1 — not because it is the easiest topic, but because it is the most foundational one.

The Three Risk Categories
💰
Category 01
Financial Risk
Errors, omissions, or deliberate manipulation that cause financial statements to misrepresent reality. Primary target of SAP GRC Access Control. Highest impact in P2P and O2C processes.

SAP Example: XK01 + F110 SoD conflict.
⚖️
Category 02
Compliance Risk
Failure to meet external regulatory obligations. Consequences include sanctions, fines, and reputational damage. Regulatory standards include SOX 404, GDPR, FDA 21 CFR Part 11, IFRS/GAAP.

Key point: SAP is the system of record — configuration gaps become compliance gaps.
⚙️
Category 03
Operational Risk
Process weaknesses, configuration errors, or human behaviour that impair accurate, efficient operations. Not necessarily fraud — but has direct financial and operational impact.

Key Definitions
Term Risk Universe
The complete set of risks identified in a client's environment before controls are considered. The starting point of every SAP controls engagement. Structured by risk category (Financial, Compliance, Operational) and by business process area (P2P, O2C, R2R, HR, etc.).
Term Inherent Risk
The level of risk present in a process or system before any mitigating controls are applied. Determined by likelihood and impact of an adverse event in the absence of controls. Your baseline on arrival at a client site.
Term Residual Risk
The risk that remains after management has implemented controls and those controls are operating effectively. Must fall within the organisation's defined risk tolerance. Measured by re-running SoD analysis or re-assessing control operating effectiveness after remediation.
Term Segregation of Duties (SoD)
A fundamental internal control principle requiring that no single individual can initiate, authorise, record, and reconcile a transaction. In SAP, SoD is enforced through role design and monitored via the GRC Access Control Ruleset. Violations are classified as Financial risk.
Term Risk and Controls Matrix (RCM)
The primary deliverable of a controls workstream. Each row maps a risk to its category, inherent rating, control, test procedure, and residual rating. Risk classification in column one drives every subsequent column. Also called a Risk Control Matrix or Controls Framework document.
Term GRC Ruleset
A library of SoD conflict rules maintained in SAP GRC Access Control. Each rule defines a pair (or group) of conflicting functions that, if held by a single user, create a financial risk. The Ruleset is effectively a codified Risk Universe for access-related risks. Standard Rulesets cover Procure-to-Pay, Order-to-Cash, Financial Accounting, HR/Payroll, and Basis/Technical domains.
Term Risk Appetite / Risk Tolerance
Risk appetite is the board-level statement of how much risk the organisation is willing to accept in pursuit of its objectives. Risk tolerance is the operational band around that appetite — the acceptable variance before escalation. In SAP GRC terms: zero unmitigated High-risk SoD conflicts in Financial Accounting is a risk tolerance threshold, not an appetite statement.
