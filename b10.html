Practitioner Capstone

Ten insights from practitioners who have done this work across multiple engagements and multiple industries. Less framework, more reflection. This is a letter from someone who has been where you are going.

"You have covered the frameworks. You know the vocabulary. You can read an FD, write a control, and navigate a testing cycle. What the frameworks do not teach you — what nobody can teach you before you have done the work — is what I am going to try to write down here. Consider this a letter from someone who has been where you are going."

Tip 01 · Starting Right
Always start with pain points.

Before you walk into your first meeting with a business process team, find out what is keeping them up at night. If you have heard there are problems with the manual journal entry threshold, lead with that. Say: I heard you have been having trouble with this — let us talk about it. The room will change. They have a hundred obligations competing for their attention. Controls is rarely at the top of that list. But when you connect to something they are already worried about, you become relevant immediately. You have something they want to talk about. The conversation you planned becomes one they want to have.

Tip 02 · Starting Right
Establish your approach before you start — and hold to it consistently.

On a complex programme, you will be working with multiple business process teams at the same time. If you tell the P2P team one thing and the Record-to-Report team something different, word will travel. They will compare notes. Inconsistency creates noise that you will then have to manage on top of everything else. Set your standard approach for how you work, what you deliver, and what you ask of them — and apply it consistently across every team from day one. Consistency is a form of credibility.

Tip 03 · Communicating Well
Over-communicate, and integrate early.

The most common reason testing phases go badly is not technical — it is that the controls requirements were not visible to the testing team until it was too late to address them properly. Tell people what you are doing and what you need from them before you need it. Integrate your controls testing requirements into the programme test cases before testing starts. Agree the evidence requirements before the first test is executed. The testing captain relationship only works if you have established it before testing begins. Over-communication is not a weakness — it is how you prevent the avoidable surprises.

Tip 04 · Communicating Well
Tailor every conversation to the person in the room.

Not everyone you work with will be literate in controls. The P2P business user who owns the three-way match control needs a different conversation than the external auditor who is going to rely on it. The CFO needs a different framing than the implementation partner's functional lead. You will have third-party vendors, external auditors, internal stakeholders, and SAP consultants — all in the same programme, all with different frames of reference. Your job is not to deliver the same explanation repeatedly until they understand it. Your job is to translate what matters into a language that makes sense for each specific person.

Tip 05 · Maintaining Rigour
Trace everything. Version your RACM from day one.

The Risk and Controls Matrix is a living document. It will go through multiple iterations from the preliminary draft in Solution Confirmation to the final version at go-live. The ability to trace from the control you tested back to the risk assessment that motivated it — and back to the original programme scope that defined the risk universe — is not just good hygiene. It is audit credibility. When the external auditor asks why a particular control exists, or why it was designed a specific way, the answer should be traceable in the RACM version history. Maintain version control from the first draft. That traceability is part of the value you deliver.

Tip 06 · Maintaining Rigour
Know your artefact landscape — and what you do when it is incomplete.

Programmes do not always give you what you need. Some clients will have business process diagrams but not functional design documents. Some will have FDs but nothing that gives you an end-to-end view of the process. When that happens, your job is to figure out how much you have to work with and what you need to get from them. Put the pieces together yourself. Build a preliminary stance on the end-to-end. Do not wait for complete documentation before forming a view — you will wait indefinitely. Work with what you have, identify the gaps explicitly, and pursue them.

Tip 07 · Maintaining Rigour
Be strategic about scope — especially in testing.

On a programme with hundreds of controls, you cannot test everything in every cycle without burning out the testing team and yourself. Apply the SIT/UAT split. Scope automated and configuration controls for system integration testing. Scope ITDM and manual controls for user acceptance testing. Be deliberate about which controls are key and which are non-key — and use the go-live period to finalise that determination based on what you have seen in testing. A rationalised, well-evidenced set of forty key controls is more valuable than three hundred controls with inconsistent evidence.

Tip 08 · Knowing Your Audience
Master the tools your programme uses — better than the implementers do.

Every programme uses some kind of tool to track testing, manage defects, and report progress. If you know how to use it better than the people running it, you get to lead the workflow instead of fitting into someone else's. That is a real advantage. It also sends a signal — that you are not just the controls person who shows up with requirements and then waits for the testing team to produce evidence. You are a full participant in how the programme runs. Whatever the tool is — learn it properly before testing starts.

Tip 09 · Building Lasting Value
Do the change management that nobody else is doing.

A go-live is not the end of a controls engagement — it is the beginning of an operational reality for the client. The people who will execute these controls from day one may have never heard of the RACM. They may not know what they are supposed to do, how often, or what happens if they miss it. You do not need to run a full change management programme — that is not your scope. But you do need to make sure the control owners know what is changing, what their responsibilities are, and what a compliant execution looks like. At minimum: clear training for ITDM and manual control owners before go-live. If they are not ready on day one, the controls are not operating on day one.

Tip 10 · Building Lasting Value
Go beyond the engagement — position yourself as a trusted advisor.

Your primary job on an engagement is to build the controls. But there is more value you can deliver than that. During the programme, surface the risks and improvement opportunities that your position in the workstream uniquely lets you see. After the programme, stay connected. What else could the controls workstream do to better support the client going forward — a more streamlined framework, a rationalised control set, an ongoing monitoring approach? The practitioners who become trusted advisors are not the ones who delivered a clean RACM and moved on. They are the ones who stayed curious about the client's situation, communicated clearly, and made themselves useful beyond the original scope.

Ten tips. Some of them will feel obvious after you have been on a few engagements. Some of them will only land when you have seen what happens when you ignore them. That is how it goes.

The work is real. The clients are real. The risks you are designing controls against are real. And the practitioner who does this work well — who understands the frameworks, communicates clearly, builds relationships, maintains rigour, and stays curious — genuinely makes organisations safer. That is worth something.

Go do good work.
