AI in Controls — Augment, Not Replace

AI agents are already showing up in the controls workstream. This module gives you a grounded picture of where they add real value, where practitioner judgement remains irreplaceable, and how the "augment not replace" frame applies to how you work.

Section 1 · What AI Agents Do in the Controls Workstream

An agent, in the controls context, is a software system that can be given a task, access relevant information, and produce a structured output — without requiring a human to do each step manually. The tasks agents handle best involve processing large volumes of structured or semi-structured information to produce a preliminary output that a practitioner then reviews and refines.

SolCon
Gap Analysis
🤖 Agent: Ingests current-state process docs + future-state programme artefacts. Surfaces which controls exist today with no future-state equivalent — and which risks are new.
Practitioner role shifts to
Reviewing the first-cut gap analysis. Validating what the agent found. Challenging what it missed. Applying programme context and professional scepticism.
Design & Build
Control Proposal
🤖 Agent: Ingests functional design documents. Proposes a control description, suggested type/nature classification, and a draft RACM entry for each FD.
Practitioner role shifts to
Reviewing each proposal. Accepting well-formed drafts. Reworking those that missed a nuance — wrong classification, overlooked error handling, manual where automated is possible.
Testing
Test Coverage Analysis
🤖 Agent: Cross-references the RACM against the test case library. Flags controls with no test coverage and test cases with evidence requirements inconsistent with the control type.
Practitioner role shifts to
Reviewing flagged gaps. Confirming whether missing coverage is intentional or an oversight. Directing the testing captain to address gaps before the testing cycle closes.
💡
The Value Is Not That the Agent Replaces Review
The value is that the practitioner starts from a structured draft rather than a blank page. On a programme with hundreds of FDs, that shift — from blank-page creation to structured review — is where the efficiency gain actually materialises. The judgement work remains. The information-processing work is accelerated.

Section 2 · The Limits of AI Capability

There are three practitioner judgements that AI agents cannot make — not as a matter of current capability, but as a matter of what the judgement requires.

⚖️ Professional Judgement

Whether a test result constitutes sufficient evidence for a specific client and engagement context. An agent can flag that evidence exists — it cannot assess whether it is appropriate for this client, this auditor, this risk level.

🔗 Client Context

Why one company code has a tolerance exception — and whether the explanation is credible. An agent can identify the exception. It cannot assess the business rationale, the relationship history, or whether the client's answer makes sense.

🧐 Professional Scepticism

Recognising when a monitoring report was signed off without any investigation of the exceptions it flagged. An agent can see the sign-off. It cannot sense the absence of substantive review behind a clean-looking form.

These assessments require a practitioner who knows the client, knows the programme, and has the professional accountability that comes with signing off on the work.

Section 3 · Augment, Not Replace — The Frame That Matters

The framing that matters is simple: AI agents propose. Practitioners decide. What changes is where in the process your attention is most needed.

Where Your Attention Shifts
Information Processing
Initial triage, first-draft analysis, volume cross-referencing — agent accelerates this
Less
Judgement
Validating, challenging, contextualising agent outputs — this grows in importance
More
Relationship Work
Client credibility, stakeholder conversations, trust — AI cannot substitute here
More
Accountability
Professional sign-off cannot be delegated to an agent — this stays with you
Same

Key Definitions
ConceptAugment Not Replace
The framing for AI in the controls workstream. Agents propose; practitioners decide. The practitioner's role does not disappear — it shifts. Less time on information processing. More time on judgement, challenge, and relationship work. Professional accountability stays with the practitioner throughout.
ConceptAgent Capability Boundary
The limit of what AI agents can do in the controls context. Agents can process volume, surface patterns, and produce structured first drafts. They cannot: assess whether evidence is sufficient for a specific engagement context; evaluate the credibility of a client's explanation; or apply the professional scepticism required to identify a sign-off that is formal but not substantive.
ConceptAttention Shift
The reallocation of practitioner effort when AI agents handle information processing. Information processing: Less (agent handles triage and first drafts). Judgement: More (validating and challenging agent outputs). Relationship work: More (client credibility cannot be automated). Accountability: Same (professional sign-off stays with the practitioner).
TermCritical Engagement (vs Passive Acceptance)
Two stances toward AI tools. Passive acceptance: receives agent outputs, accepts without challenge, misses errors agents cannot self-detect. Critical engagement: develops a working understanding of AI capability, reviews outputs with scepticism, applies professional judgement where required. Practitioners who engage critically will be better positioned than those who ignore AI entirely or defer to it uncritically.
